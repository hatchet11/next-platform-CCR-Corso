## Breeding a Cane Corso the Right Way

Breeding Cane Corsos is a serious undertaking. Done well, it preserves everything exceptional about this breed — the loyalty, the structure, the temperament, the health. Done carelessly, it contributes to the suffering of individual dogs and the degradation of the breed as a whole.

This guide is for breeders and serious enthusiasts who want to understand how ethical Cane Corso breeding actually works — from selecting the right dogs to delivering healthy puppies.

---

## Step 1: Breeding Stock Selection

The foundation of every litter is the quality of the parents. Both the sire and dam must meet the breed standard in structure, movement, and — critically — temperament.

**What to look for in breeding candidates:**

- **Temperament:** Calm, confident, stable. The Cane Corso standard calls for a dog that is intelligent, easily trained, docile, and affectionate with its family. Dogs that are fearful, reactive, or unpredictably aggressive should never be bred. These traits are heritable — you will pass them on.
- **Structure:** Correct angulation, strong topline, proper head type. Movement should be effortless and efficient.
- **Age:** Females should not be bred before their second heat cycle or 18–24 months of age. Males should be fully mature before being used at stud.

**Titles and evaluations add value.** OFA certifications, AKC conformation titles, working titles (IGP, PSA), and temperament evaluations all provide objective data on the breeding candidate.

## Step 2: Genetic Health Testing

No breeding should proceed without health testing. This protects the puppies, the buyers, and the breed.

### Dental-Skeletal-Retinal Anomaly (DSRA)
DSRA is a serious inherited disorder specific to Cane Corsos that affects the teeth, skeleton, and eyes. It is caused by a recessive gene mutation — meaning two copies (one from each parent) are required for a puppy to be affected.

**The math matters:**
- Two carriers bred together produce a 25% chance of affected puppies
- One carrier + one clear dog produces no affected puppies (though some may be carriers)
- Two clear dogs produce no affected puppies

DNA testing identifies: **Clear**, **Carrier**, or **Affected**. Both breeding dogs should be DNA tested before pairing. At minimum, at least one parent should be DNA clear.

### OFA Certifications Required Before Breeding
- **Hips** — OFA or PennHIP evaluation (dysplasia is heritable)
- **Elbows** — OFA elbow evaluation
- **Cardiac** — OFA cardiac exam by board-certified cardiologist (for DCM)
- **Eyes** — OFA CAER exam (for PRA and eyelid conditions)

A reputable breeder tests all four before every breeding. Results should be publicly available on the OFA website.

## Step 3: Progesterone Testing and Breeding Timing

The most common reason a breeding fails is incorrect timing. Progesterone testing is the single most reliable tool for identifying when a female is actually ready to conceive.

### Understanding the Cycle

The female's cycle moves through stages:
1. **Proestrus** — vaginal bleeding, not yet ready to breed
2. **Estrus** — ovulation occurs, optimal breeding window
3. **Diestrus** — post-ovulation; pregnancy or false pregnancy
4. **Anestrus** — rest phase

External signs (behavior, swelling, discharge) are unreliable. Progesterone levels don't lie.

### Progesterone Thresholds

| Level | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| < 1 ng/mL | Proestrus; not yet approaching ovulation |
| ~1 ng/mL | LH surge beginning |
| 2–5 ng/mL | Ovulation occurring or imminent |
| 10–40 ng/mL | Peak luteal phase; optimal breeding window (typically 48–72 hours post-ovulation) |

Testing every 2–3 days once initial rise is detected allows you to catch the optimal window. Miss it, and the entire cycle is wasted.

**Natural breeding or artificial insemination (AI):** Both are used in Corsos. Chilled or frozen AI allows breeding to distant studs and requires even more precise progesterone timing.

## Step 4: Pregnancy and Prenatal Care

Canine gestation is approximately **63 days from ovulation** (not from breeding date).

**Prenatal care:**
- Ultrasound at day 25–30 to confirm pregnancy and estimate litter size
- X-ray at day 55–58 to count skulls and confirm puppy count (critical for knowing when whelping is complete)
- Maintain the female on high-quality food; transition to puppy food in the final third of pregnancy
- Keep exercise moderate — daily walks are fine, avoid intense activity after week 6
- No dewormers, vaccines, or unnecessary medications during pregnancy without veterinary guidance

## Step 5: Whelping Preparation

Cane Corsos are a large, deep-chested breed with documented whelping challenges. Small litters (1–3 puppies) have significantly higher rates of dystocia — difficult labor requiring intervention — than larger litters.

### Whelping Box Setup
- Large enough for the dam to fully stretch out
- Low sides that newborns can't fall over, high enough to contain them
- Pig rails (rails around the perimeter) to prevent the dam from crushing puppies against the walls
- Heat lamp or whelping pad to maintain temperature (90°F for newborns, reduced as puppies develop)
- Clean, replaceable bedding

### Supplies to Have Ready
- Digital thermometer (dam's temperature drops below 99°F within 24 hours of labor)
- Clean towels and gloves
- Bulb syringe (clear airways)
- Dental floss or umbilical clamp (tie off cords if needed)
- Iodine (for cord sterilization)
- Whelping scale (weigh every puppy at birth and daily)
- Emergency vet contact — a relationship with a vet who does emergency C-sections is essential

### Whelping Timeline
- Labor in Corsos typically produces one puppy every 30–60 minutes
- **Intervals exceeding 2 hours between puppies significantly increase stillbirth risk**
- If active straining lasts more than 30–60 minutes without delivery, contact your vet
- Know your planned puppy count (from the X-ray) so you know when whelping is complete

Do not leave a laboring Cane Corso unattended. Attend every birth.

## Step 6: Newborn Puppy Care (0–8 Weeks)

### Days 0–14: Neonatal Period
- Puppies are born blind and deaf; entirely dependent on dam
- Verify every puppy nurses within the first hour (colostrum is critical for immunity)
- Weigh daily — puppies should gain steadily; failure to gain is an emergency
- Keep environment 85–90°F; puppies cannot regulate body temperature
- Watch for fading puppy syndrome (hypothermia, hypoglycemia, failure to nurse)

### Weeks 2–4: Transitional Period
- Eyes and ears open
- Begin introducing soft puppy mush at 3–4 weeks
- Start handling: daily gentle handling by different people establishes tolerance

### Weeks 4–8: Socialization Window Opens
- The critical socialization period begins in earnest
- Expose puppies to: different surfaces, sounds, people of varied ages and appearances
- Begin basic neurological stimulation exercises (Early Neurological Stimulation / ENS) if not already done from day 3
- Deworm according to veterinary schedule (typically at 2, 4, 6, and 8 weeks)
- First vaccinations at 6–8 weeks

**At CCR Kennels**, we begin socialization, handling, and ENS from the first days of life. Every puppy leaves us with a foundation that matters.

## Responsible Breeding Is a Commitment

Breeding a Cane Corso litter is a minimum of 8–12 weeks of intensive work, preceded by months of health testing and planning. The decisions you make — which dogs to breed, what tests to run, how you raise the puppies — ripple through the lives of every puppy you produce and every family that trusts you with one.

This is why reputable breeders health test, socialize, stand behind their puppies with guarantees, and stay involved with buyers for the life of the dog.
