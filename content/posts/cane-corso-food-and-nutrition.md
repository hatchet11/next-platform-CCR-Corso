## Feeding a Cane Corso: Why It Matters More Than You Think

Nutrition is one of the most important factors in your Cane Corso's health, longevity, joint function, coat quality, and energy levels. For a breed that can reach 120+ pounds and live 10–12 years, what you feed from puppyhood forward has real consequences.

This guide breaks down what Cane Corsos need at each life stage, what to look for in a food, and what to avoid.

---

## Cane Corso Nutritional Needs

Cane Corsos are large, muscular, athletic dogs. Their diet should reflect that:

- **High-quality animal protein** — the primary ingredient and caloric driver
- **Moderate fat** — for energy, coat health, and fat-soluble vitamins
- **Controlled calcium and phosphorus** — especially important in puppies to support healthy bone growth without accelerating it
- **Joint-supporting nutrients** — glucosamine, chondroitin, and omega-3 fatty acids
- **Limited fillers** — corn, soy, and wheat provide poor nutritional value relative to their caloric content

---

## Feeding Cane Corso Puppies (8 Weeks – 18 Months)

**This is the most critical feeding period.**

Large breed puppies have unique nutritional needs that differ significantly from small breeds. The goal is controlled, steady growth — not rapid growth. Puppies that grow too fast develop bones and joints that can't keep up with their body weight, increasing the risk of hip dysplasia, elbow issues, and other orthopedic problems.

### What to Look For in a Puppy Food

- **AAFCO statement:** "Complete and balanced for growth of large breed puppies" or "all life stages including large breed puppies"
- **First ingredient:** A named animal protein (chicken, beef, lamb, salmon — not "meat meal" or generic "animal protein")
- **Calcium content:** 1.0–1.8% on a dry matter basis
- **Phosphorus:** 0.8–1.6% on a dry matter basis
- **Caloric density:** Moderate — avoid very high-calorie puppy foods

### Puppy Feeding Schedule

- **8–12 weeks:** 3–4 meals per day
- **3–6 months:** 3 meals per day
- **6–12 months:** 2 meals per day
- **12–18 months:** Transition to adult food

### Portion Guidance

Follow the feeding guidelines on the bag as a starting point, then adjust based on body condition. You should be able to feel your puppy's ribs without pressing hard, but not see them. A Corso puppy should look lean — not chubby.

## Feeding Adult Cane Corsos (18 Months – 7 Years)

Once your Corso reaches adulthood, switch to a large breed adult formula. Protein requirements remain high; fat can be slightly reduced compared to puppyhood.

### Daily Amounts (General Guidance)

| Weight | Cups Per Day (Approximate) |
|--------|---------------------------|
| 80 lbs | 4–5 cups |
| 100 lbs | 5–6 cups |
| 120 lbs | 6–7 cups |

Adjust based on activity level. A working or highly active Corso may need more; a less active dog may need less. Obesity is a major health risk in large breeds — monitor body condition monthly.

### Two Meals Per Day

Always feed adult Corsos twice daily rather than once. This reduces the risk of bloat (GDV — see below) and keeps blood sugar and energy levels more stable.

## Bloat Prevention in Cane Corsos

**Bloat (Gastric Dilatation-Volvulus, or GDV) is a life-threatening emergency** that large, deep-chested breeds like the Cane Corso are predisposed to. The stomach fills with gas and can twist, cutting off blood supply.

**To reduce bloat risk:**

- Feed two smaller meals instead of one large one
- Avoid exercise for 1–2 hours before and after meals
- Use a slow-feeder bowl if your dog eats quickly
- Avoid raised food bowls (research is inconclusive, but elevated bowls may increase risk in some dogs)
- Consider prophylactic gastropexy (stomach tacking) when your dog is spayed or neutered — ask your vet

Know the signs of bloat: distended abdomen, unproductive retching, excessive drooling, restlessness. This is a veterinary emergency — act immediately.

## Feeding Senior Cane Corsos (7+ Years)

As your Corso ages, metabolism slows and activity levels typically decrease. Senior-specific considerations:

- **Reduce calories** if weight gain occurs
- **Maintain protein** — seniors need adequate protein to preserve muscle mass
- **Add joint support** — glucosamine and chondroitin become increasingly important
- **Omega-3 fatty acids** — support joint, heart, and cognitive health (fish oil supplementation is often beneficial)
- **Regular vet check-ins** — senior dogs benefit from twice-yearly exams

---

## Raw Feeding and Home-Cooked Diets

Some Cane Corso owners choose raw (BARF — Biologically Appropriate Raw Food) or home-cooked diets. These can be excellent options when done correctly, but require careful attention to nutritional balance.

**If you pursue raw feeding:**
- Research calcium-to-phosphorus ratios carefully — especially for puppies
- Source high-quality, human-grade ingredients
- Consider consulting a veterinary nutritionist
- Follow safe food handling practices to protect both your dog and your household

**If you prefer commercial kibble:** That's completely fine. High-quality commercial foods are nutritionally complete and convenient. The "best" diet is the one that fits your lifestyle and that your dog thrives on.

---

## Ingredients to Avoid

- Artificial preservatives (BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin)
- Artificial colors and flavors
- Generic "meat meal" or "animal by-products" as primary ingredients
- Excessive corn, wheat, or soy as main ingredients
- Foods with multiple types of grain listed in the first five ingredients

---

## Recommended Supplements for Cane Corsos

- **Fish oil (omega-3):** Supports joints, coat, heart, and brain
- **Glucosamine + chondroitin:** Joint health, especially as the dog ages
- **Probiotics:** Digestive health support
- **Vitamin E:** Antioxidant support (especially if feeding fish oil)

Always consult your veterinarian before adding supplements, particularly with puppies.
