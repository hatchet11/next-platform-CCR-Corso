## Cane Corso Health: The Full Picture

The Cane Corso is generally a robust breed — but like all purebreds, it carries predispositions to certain health conditions. Understanding these conditions helps you choose a responsible breeder, monitor your dog proactively, and make informed decisions throughout its life.

This guide covers the most significant health concerns in the breed, what causes them, what to watch for, and how responsible breeding reduces risk.

---

## Hip and Elbow Dysplasia

**What it is:** Dysplasia refers to abnormal development of a joint — specifically the hip ball-and-socket joint or the elbow joint. When the joint doesn't develop correctly, the surfaces don't fit properly, leading to instability, wear, inflammation, and eventually arthritis.

**Why Corsos are at risk:** Large breeds carry more body weight over their joints, increasing the mechanical stress on hip and elbow structures. Genetics play a major role in predisposition.

**Signs to watch for:**
- Reluctance to rise from lying down
- Stiffness after exercise
- Bunny hopping (using both rear legs together rather than alternating)
- Reduced range of motion in the hips or elbows
- Muscle loss in the hindquarters

**Prevention and management:**
- Buy from health-tested parents (OFA or PennHIP hip ratings; OFA elbow ratings)
- Controlled growth in puppyhood — avoid overfeeding and high-impact activity before 18 months
- Maintain healthy body weight throughout life (excess weight accelerates joint damage)
- Joint supplements (glucosamine, chondroitin, omega-3s) support long-term joint health
- Surgical options exist for severe cases — consult a veterinary orthopedic specialist

## Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)

**What it is:** DCM is a disease of the heart muscle in which the heart becomes enlarged and weakened, reducing its ability to pump blood effectively. In advanced cases, it can lead to congestive heart failure.

**Why Corsos are at risk:** Large and giant breeds have a higher incidence of DCM than smaller dogs. Genetic factors are involved, though diet (particularly grain-free, legume-heavy diets) has also been associated with DCM in some dogs.

**Signs to watch for:**
- Lethargy and reduced exercise tolerance
- Coughing, especially at night
- Labored breathing or rapid breathing at rest
- Sudden collapse or fainting
- Distended abdomen

**Prevention and screening:**
- Annual cardiac exams performed by a board-certified cardiologist (OFA Cardiac)
- Purchase from breeders who cardiac-screen their dogs
- Avoid high-legume grain-free diets (ongoing research; consult your vet)
- Regular veterinary check-ups including cardiac auscultation

**Important:** DCM can be present without obvious symptoms. Annual cardiac screening catches it earlier, when management is more effective.

## Bloat (Gastric Dilatation-Volvulus / GDV)

**What it is:** GDV occurs when the stomach fills with gas and rotates on its axis, trapping the gas inside and cutting off blood supply. It is a life-threatening emergency that can kill within hours without intervention.

**Why Corsos are at risk:** Deep-chested breeds are significantly more prone to GDV. The anatomy of a large, deep chest allows the stomach more room to twist.

**Signs — act immediately if you see these:**
- Distended, hard abdomen
- Unproductive retching (trying to vomit but nothing comes up)
- Excessive drooling
- Restlessness, pacing, inability to get comfortable
- Pale gums

**This is a veterinary emergency. Go immediately — do not wait.**

**Prevention:**
- Feed two smaller meals daily rather than one large meal
- Restrict activity 1–2 hours before and after meals
- Use a slow-feeder bowl if your dog eats quickly
- Consider prophylactic gastropexy (a surgical procedure that tacks the stomach to prevent rotation) when your dog is spayed or neutered — ask your vet

## Eye Conditions

Cane Corsos are prone to several hereditary eye conditions:

### Entropion
The eyelid rolls inward, causing the eyelashes to rub against the cornea. Signs include excessive tearing, squinting, and eye discharge. Surgical correction is straightforward and effective.

### Ectropion
The eyelid rolls outward, exposing the inner eyelid. Can cause chronic irritation and infection. Mild cases may be managed medically; severe cases require surgery.

### Cherry Eye
The gland of the third eyelid prolapses and becomes visible as a red mass in the inner corner of the eye. Surgical repositioning (not removal) is the recommended treatment.

### Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA)
A hereditary condition causing gradual deterioration of the retina, eventually leading to blindness. DNA testing can identify carriers before breeding.

**Screening:** OFA CAER (Companion Animal Eye Registry) exams, performed by a board-certified veterinary ophthalmologist, are recommended for breeding dogs.

## Mange (Demodectic)

**What it is:** Demodex mites are normal residents of dog skin. In dogs with compromised immune systems, these mites can overpopulate, causing hair loss, skin thickening, and secondary infections.

**Signs:**
- Patchy hair loss, often starting around the face and eyes
- Skin may appear red, scaly, or thickened
- Secondary bacterial infections in severe cases

**Management:** Demodex is generally treatable with veterinary supervision. Severe or recurrent cases may indicate underlying immune system issues. Dogs with generalized demodex should generally not be bred.

## Orthopedic Developmental Diseases

In addition to hip and elbow dysplasia, growing Cane Corsos can be affected by:

- **Osteochondrosis (OCD):** Defective cartilage development in joints, often affecting the shoulder
- **Panosteitis:** Bone inflammation in adolescent large breeds causing shifting-leg lameness; typically self-resolving
- **Hypertrophic Osteodystrophy (HOD):** Painful bone condition in puppies, often related to rapid growth and nutrition

Controlled growth through appropriate diet and moderate exercise significantly reduces the risk of these conditions.

---

## Hypothyroidism

The thyroid gland regulates metabolism, energy, and coat quality. Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) causes:

- Weight gain despite normal food intake
- Lethargy
- Poor coat quality and hair loss
- Cold intolerance

Diagnosed via blood test. Managed effectively with daily oral medication.

---

## Idiopathic Epilepsy

Seizures with no identifiable structural cause occur in some Cane Corsos. Idiopathic epilepsy has a genetic component. Management includes anticonvulsant medication and regular veterinary monitoring.

## How to Reduce Your Cane Corso's Health Risks

1. **Buy from a health-tested breeder** — ask for OFA results on hips, elbows, heart, and eyes
2. **Feed appropriately for each life stage** — controlled growth in puppies, weight management in adults
3. **Maintain a healthy body weight** — obesity accelerates joint disease, cardiac disease, and metabolic problems
4. **Annual veterinary exams** — catch problems early when management is most effective
5. **Bi-annual exams for seniors** — dogs 7+ benefit from twice-yearly check-ups
6. **Know the emergency signs** — especially bloat (GDV), which can kill in hours
