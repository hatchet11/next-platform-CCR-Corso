3:T12fd,<h2>Getting Ready for Go-Home Day</h2>
<p>Bringing home a Cane Corso puppy is one of the most exciting things you will do as a dog owner. These powerful, loyal dogs bond deeply with their families — but they also require proper preparation to thrive from day one.</p>
<p>At CCR Kennels, we send every puppy home with a full care package, but your home environment matters just as much as what is in the bag. Here is what we recommend you have ready before go-home day.</p>
<h2>Essential Supplies</h2>
<p><strong>Crate and bedding</strong>
Cane Corsos grow fast. A puppy that fits in a small crate at 8 weeks will be over 100 pounds within a year. We recommend purchasing a large or extra-large wire crate with a divider panel so you can adjust the space as your puppy grows. Add a machine-washable fleece mat or crate pad — avoid foam beds until your puppy is past the chewing stage.</p>
<p><strong>Food and water bowls</strong>
Choose stainless steel or ceramic bowls — they are easier to sanitize and do not harbor bacteria the way plastic bowls can. Elevated feeders are popular with large breeds, though current veterinary consensus on bloat risk is mixed. Ask your vet for guidance.</p>
<p><strong>Collar, leash, and ID tag</strong>
You will want a flat collar with a breakaway or quick-release buckle and a 6-foot leather or nylon leash. Have an ID tag engraved with your phone number before your puppy arrives. A second backup tag is not a bad idea.</p>
<p><strong>Puppy food</strong>
We will tell you exactly what food your puppy has been eating so you can continue the same diet. Switching foods abruptly causes digestive upset — if you plan to transition to a different food, do it gradually over 7–10 days by mixing old and new.</p>
<p><strong>Enzyme cleaner</strong>
Accidents will happen. Keep a bottle of enzymatic cleaner on hand. Standard household cleaners do not fully break down urine proteins — enzyme-based products eliminate the scent markers that cause puppies to return to the same spot.</p>
<h2>Puppy-Proofing Your Home</h2>
<p>Cane Corso puppies are curious and mouthy. Before your puppy comes home, walk through each room at puppy-nose height and think about what is accessible.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Electrical cords</strong> — bundle them or run them behind furniture, or use cord covers</li>
<li><strong>Toxic plants</strong> — common houseplants like pothos, philodendron, and sago palm are toxic to dogs; move them out of reach or remove them</li>
<li><strong>Cabinets and trash cans</strong> — use child-proof latches on lower cabinets containing cleaning products or food; get a trash can with a locking lid</li>
<li><strong>Stairs</strong> — decide whether you want to gate off stairs while your puppy's joints are still developing (recommended under 12 months for large breeds)</li>
<li><strong>Small objects</strong> — hair ties, coins, socks, and children's toys are choking hazards</li>
</ul>
<h2>Setting Up a Safe Space</h2>
<p>Your puppy needs a quiet, designated space to decompress. A crate in a low-traffic area of your home — but not completely isolated — works best. Puppies that feel secure in a crate are easier to house-train and experience less separation anxiety as adults.</p>
<p>Place the crate near a family area so your puppy can see and hear people without being overwhelmed. Cover the top and sides with a blanket to create a den-like environment.</p>
<h2>Yard and Outdoor Safety</h2>
<p>If you have a yard, inspect your fence before your puppy comes home.</p>
<ul>
<li>Check for gaps at the base — Cane Corso puppies are surprisingly good at squeezing through small openings</li>
<li>Ensure gates have secure latches</li>
<li>Identify and remove any toxic plants from garden beds</li>
<li>Remove standing water to reduce mosquito exposure</li>
</ul>
<h2>Your First Veterinary Visit</h2>
<p>Schedule a vet appointment within 3–5 days of bringing your puppy home. Your puppy will come to you with a health record and vaccination history — bring this with you. Your vet will confirm current vaccine status and set up the rest of the puppy series.</p>
<p>We strongly recommend establishing a relationship with a vet who has experience with large and giant breeds. Cane Corsos have different growth curves and joint considerations than smaller dogs.</p>
<h2>One Last Thing</h2>
<p>The most important thing you can bring home is patience. Your puppy is leaving its mother, littermates, and the only environment it has ever known. The first few nights may involve some crying — this is completely normal. A warm water bottle wrapped in a towel, placed in the crate, can help simulate the warmth of a littermate.</p>
<p>We are always available to answer questions before and after go-home day. That support does not end at pickup — it is part of every CCR Kennels family.</p>
